    Tensile Tests on Extracellular Matrix Model

    Skills Used: Linux, Python, LAMMPs

    During my tenure at UCONN Research, I was part of a specialized team entrusted with the pivotal task of formulating a precise computational model of the extracellular matrix. This undertaking was strategically aimed at providing a robust framework for prospective investigations into the realm of rare diseases. Given the inherent intricacy of the extracellular matrix, characterized by its minuscule scale, our approach necessitated the utilization of computational models as the most efficacious means of in-depth structural scrutiny. Within the scope of this research endeavor, I assumed the role of refining the tensile strength attributes inherent to the extracellular matrix.

    To commence this effort, I embarked on an extensive exploration of academic studies, delving into the behavior of tensile strength exhibited by the extracellular matrix. My inquiry led me to the discerning realization that the ideal representation of the extracellular matrix's structural attributes is akin to that of a web-like configuration.

    Two real life images that demonstrate the web-like structure that is the ECM

    To effectively execute this project, I undertook a multifaceted approach, involving the acquisition of proficiencies in Linux and LAMMPs, while simultaneously harnessing my existing expertise in MatLab and Python. It is noteworthy to mention that LAMMPs is most optimally executed within the Linux operating environment. Consequently, I constructed LAMMPs code, meticulously aligning thousands of simulated particles in a web-like formation.

    Here are four web structure simulations with varying impact locations and speeds: 15 m/s on the lower left, 30 m/s on the upper right, 35 m/s at the center, and 50 m/s at the center.

    Leveraging data gathered from both these simulations and a variety of other experiments involving web-like structures, I created a data-driven model shown in the video below. In this model, I utilized the properties observed in the earlier simulation to generate a system where individual beads interact as if connected by a web-like string, even though the overall appearance may not resemble a traditional web.

    I developed a Python script capable of processing the output data generated by LAMMPs simulations. This script was employed to generate a stress-strain graph based on the simulation results. The image outputted a stress strain ratio that emulates that of a real spiderweb, meaning that the project was succesful and could be passed on to the next person in my research group.

    Project One: Cooperative Enzymes

    Background: in cooperative enzymatic reactions, when one substrate binds to the enzyme, it enhances the receptivity of other binding sites because the enzyme undergoes chemical modifications.

    Substrate Concentration vs Reaction Velocity

    Above is a graph that represents the substrate concentration vs reaction velocity in a cooperative enzymatic reaction. The standard for a cooperative reaction is a resemblance of a sinusoidal curve, as seen above. My first goal was to use the data below and determine whether or not I would get a similar curve.

    S (mM) V (mM/s)

    0.5 0.06
    1.0 0.27
    1.5 0.50
    2.0 0.67
    2.5 0.78
    3.5 0.89
    4.0 0.92
    4.5 0.94
    5.0 0.95


    This data generated the sinusoidal curve below, but this was not enough to determine whether the reaction was cooperative, and more complex data manipulation was required.
